What is GTO Strategy in Poker and Why Should You Know It?

Strategy · 8 min read

What Does GTO Mean in Poker?

GTO stands for Game Theory Optimal — a poker strategy based on mathematical game theory. A GTO strategy is one where you play in such a perfectly balanced way that no opponent can exploit you, no matter what they do. Even if your opponent knew your exact strategy, they could not find a way to profit against it.

GTO is not a specific set of rules or a list of moves to memorize. It is a theoretical framework — an ideal that guides decision-making. Think of it as the "perfect" way to play poker, calculated by powerful computers called solvers.

In practical terms, GTO poker means mixing your actions (raise, call, fold, check) at mathematically correct frequencies so that your opponents cannot figure out what you are holding based on your actions. If you always raise with strong hands and fold with weak ones, a good opponent will read you easily. GTO prevents this by making you unpredictable.

Why is GTO Important for Poker Players?

Understanding GTO is important for three reasons:

  • It gives you a baseline strategy that cannot be beaten. When you do not know your opponents, playing close to GTO ensures you are not making major mistakes. You might not maximize profit, but you will not get exploited either.
  • It helps you understand when opponents deviate. If you know what the "correct" play is, you can spot when someone plays differently — and adjust to exploit them.
  • It improves every part of your game. Studying GTO forces you to think about hand ranges, frequencies, and bet sizing in a structured way. This deeper understanding carries over to all your poker decisions.

The rise of online poker and solver technology has made GTO knowledge essential. Twenty years ago, a good player could win by reading opponents and playing tight. Today, even mid-stakes games are filled with players who understand GTO principles. Without at least a basic grasp of GTO, you are at a significant disadvantage in modern poker.

GTO vs Exploitative Play — What is the Difference?

There are two fundamental approaches to poker strategy:

GTO (balanced) play focuses on making yourself unexploitable. You mix your actions at theoretically correct frequencies. For example, on a certain river spot, GTO might tell you to bet 70% of the time and check 30% — with a specific mix of value hands and bluffs. This balance ensures your opponent cannot gain an edge whether they call, fold, or raise.

Exploitative play means deliberately deviating from GTO to take advantage of a specific opponent's mistakes. If you notice someone folds too often to river bets, you bluff more. If someone calls too much, you bluff less and value bet thinner. Exploitative play generates more profit against weak players, but it also makes you predictable — and vulnerable if your opponent adjusts.

In practice, the best poker players use both approaches:

  • They start with a GTO foundation — especially against unknown opponents or strong players.
  • They switch to exploitative adjustments when they identify clear tendencies.
  • They return to GTO when an opponent starts adjusting back.

Think of GTO as your "default mode." It is the strategy you fall back on when you have no specific reads. Exploitative play is your "attack mode" — used when you spot a weakness to exploit.

How Do Poker Solvers Work?

A poker solver is software that calculates GTO solutions for specific poker scenarios. Popular solvers include PioSOLVER, GTO+, and MonkerSolver.

Here is how they work in simple terms:

  • You input a specific poker situation — the positions, stack sizes, board cards, and allowed bet sizes.
  • The solver runs millions of simulations, with two "players" playing against each other.
  • Each player tries to exploit the other's strategy. The solver keeps adjusting both strategies until neither player can improve.
  • The result is a Nash Equilibrium — a set of strategies where both players are playing optimally against each other.

The output is a set of frequencies: for example, "raise A♠K♣ 85% of the time, call 15% of the time from this position against this bet size." These frequencies represent the GTO strategy for that specific spot.

Important note: solvers calculate solutions for specific situations, not general rules. The GTO play with AK on a K♠7♣2♦ board is different from AK on a J♠T♠9♣ board. This is why fully "playing GTO" is humanly impossible — there are too many unique situations. What players do instead is learn the patterns and principles behind solver outputs.

Key GTO Concepts Every Player Should Know

Balanced Ranges

A balanced range contains both value hands (hands you bet for profit) and bluffs in a mathematically correct ratio. If you only bet with strong hands, opponents will always fold and you lose value. If you only bluff, opponents will always call and catch you. GTO balances these so opponents are indifferent — calling and folding are equally profitable against you.

Mixed Frequencies

In GTO poker, you do not always take the same action with the same hand. A solver might tell you to raise with Q♠J♠ 60% of the time and call 40% of the time in a specific spot. This mixing makes it impossible for opponents to put you on a hand based on your action alone. Humans approximate this by using randomization — for example, looking at the second hand of your watch to decide.

Minimum Defense Frequency (MDF)

MDF answers the question: "How often should I call or raise to prevent my opponent from profitably bluffing with any two cards?" The formula is simple: MDF = pot size / (pot size + bet size). Against a half-pot bet, MDF is 67% — you need to continue with at least 67% of your range to stop automatic bluff profitability. This is one of the most practical GTO concepts you can apply immediately.

Pot Geometry and Bet Sizing

GTO strategy uses specific bet sizes for specific situations. Smaller bets are used when ranges are close together (both players could have similar hands). Larger bets are used when ranges are polarized (the bettor has either very strong hands or bluffs, nothing in between). Understanding why GTO uses certain sizes helps you choose better sizes in your own play.

GTO Preflop Strategy — Where to Start

For most players, the highest-impact application of GTO is preflop play. Preflop decisions are the most common — you make one every single hand — and they set up everything that follows.

GTO preflop strategy covers:

  • Opening ranges by position — which hands to raise with from each seat. UTG opens tight (~13% of hands), the Button opens wide (~43%). These ranges are well-established and form the backbone of any solid strategy.
  • 3-bet ranges — which hands to re-raise with when someone has already raised. GTO 3-bet ranges include both value hands (like AA, KK, AK) and bluffs (like A5s, A4s) to stay balanced.
  • Calling vs 3-bet — which hands to call with, and which to fold, when facing a 3-bet. This depends on your position, the 3-bettor's position, and stack depth.
  • Squeeze plays — 3-betting when there is a raiser and a caller in front of you. GTO squeeze ranges are tighter than regular 3-bet ranges because you face more opponents.

Common Misconceptions About GTO Poker

"GTO means always playing the same way"

The opposite is true. GTO requires mixing your actions — sometimes raising, sometimes calling, sometimes folding with the exact same hand. It is the most varied style of play possible.

"GTO is only for high stakes"

GTO principles apply at every level. In fact, learning GTO preflop ranges has the biggest impact at low and mid stakes, where most players deviate wildly from correct play. Knowing the baseline makes your deviations intentional rather than random.

"You need a solver to play GTO"

Solvers are tools for studying GTO off the table. At the table, you use the patterns and principles you have learned. Nobody runs a solver in real time during a hand. Simplified GTO charts — like those in our preflop advisor — are how players actually apply GTO in practice.

"GTO does not work in live poker"

GTO works everywhere because it is the mathematically correct baseline. In live poker, where players tend to make bigger mistakes, you might deviate more toward exploitative play — but your GTO knowledge is what tells you where the deviations are.

How to Study GTO Effectively

The best approach to learning GTO follows this progression:

  • Step 1: Master preflop ranges. Learn the correct opening, 3-betting, and calling ranges for each position. Use a preflop chart or tool daily until the ranges feel automatic.
  • Step 2: Learn common postflop patterns. Study how solvers play common board textures — paired boards, monotone boards, connected boards. Focus on patterns, not memorization.
  • Step 3: Understand bet sizing logic. Learn why GTO uses small bets on some boards and large bets on others. This will transform your postflop game.
  • Step 4: Practice in play. Apply what you have learned in real games. Start with preflop, then gradually add postflop concepts.

Remember: GTO mastery is a journey, not a destination. Even professional players continue studying solver outputs after years of play. The goal is not perfection — it is continuous improvement.
